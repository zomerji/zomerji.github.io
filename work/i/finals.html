V Finals

The first final is of the individual who would finish second in the Elites race (and by a tenth of a second), posing whilst on the move. I chose this picture because it expresses an irony; a more informal and casual attitude against the individual’s (expected) strong emotion and suffering in the moment through fatigue and lactic acid effect. The second is of a line of the racers well into the race. I decided to go for less of a mimicry of Ellis’ work here; I chose this to have an underlying meaning to it, which corresponds to the pace-setting section of the race that the photo was taken in. I won’t reveal the actual meaning, but instead I’ll leave it for you to figure out. The third is of the peloton just past the start and finish line, and I intentionally wished to depict the reaction of one of the participant’s families as the group went past. I don’t know which rider had the ‘audience’, but what matters is that I managed to capture authentic emotions. However, the image required motion blurring for cinematic effect, as the shutter speed was set at 1/800. The fourth is of a passing-by cyclist who decided to take a few photos, and so I took that opportunity to capture my own which represents the contrast between the spectator/enthusiast and their kindled interest, and the pro/semi-pro and their profession. It took a few attempts to get the composition just right. The fifth is an image my cousin took of me on his bike that I edited. I am in the IAB position, which is something that the pros tend to do in world tour races, but since I am in low-end clothing and on low-end equipment, the photograph overall symbolises aspirations to succeed in a sport, through determination and persistence. Not much editing has been done here, besides colour and tone adjustment. Therefore, it is more ‘natural’. The sixth is my own bike stood in contrast against the racers on theirs, which is primarily a return to the ‘aspiration’ theme, and secondarily an anthropomorphism of the former aspect. The seventh is of a steward ringing ‘the bell’ at the start/finish line, which is used to inform the participants that it is the last lap. I am glad that the composition came out ideally (as my camera, limited to three frames per second and with a half-frame sensor, struggled to keep up throughout the race), although a higher aperture to have the man in focus would have been preferred. The eighth is of another photographer taking pictures of the peloton, and with this I wanted to combine the theme of sport photography with the more almost-surrealist perspective of Andreas Gursky; a demonstration of sorts. I edited the tones and colours carefully, and removed anything that didn’t need to exist there. The ninth is the finish of the race, the most important part of it, and unfortunately the closest shot to the actual line that I got (the next frame was the riders over the line). However, it was workable, and so I cropped it and adjusted the tones, colours, and other factors, to increase the dramatic and cinematic effect. The tenth is my favourite photo of this shoot, and depicts the runner-up and his teammate. This was by far the shot with the most authentic emotion in it, and the underlying meaning to it is too elabourate to express here. I edited the photograph with tonal and colour adjustment, background blurring, and other changes.
